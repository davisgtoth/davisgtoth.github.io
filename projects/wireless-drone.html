Objective

Is it possible to achieve flight without active control? This project explores this question by engineering a drone that is wirelessly powered and self-stabilizing. The drone is designed without batteries, sensors, or microcontrollers, relying on strictly passive components. This constraint shifts the burden of stability from software to physics. By shaping the transmitting magnetic field into a potential well, the system generates a natural restoring force, allowing for passive control.

Theory

Approached from first principles, the system relies on Faraday's Law and resonant inductive coupling between a transmitting (TX) LC tank and four receiving (RX) tanks. The oscillating magnetic field induces an AC voltage in each receiver coil which is subsequently rectified to drive the drone's motors, generating the lift necessary for flight.

Stability is achieved through a quadcopter configuration and the geometry of the magnetic field. The transmitter creates a 'magnetic well' which has a position-dependent power gradient. When the drone drifts, the coil entering the stronger field region generates more thrust, creating a restoring torque that naturally pushes the drone back to equilibrium.

Simulation

With a theoretical framework established, simulations were conducted to explore the complex dynamics and coupling within the system. A Python-based LTI model was created to analyze how variations in coupling due to flight dynamics impacts power transfer and resonance stability. The system's sensitivity to the RX load was also explored.

Additionally, the circuitry was modeled in LTspice to quantify power transfer and the expected frequency response. This was critical for characterizing the impact of the full-wave bridge rectifier, an inherently non-linear component of the system.

Test Stand

The simulations demonstrated a strong dependence on the RX load, so given the complexity of modelling a motor, a shift towards rapid physical prototyping was necessary. A custom test stand was designed and 3D-printed to empirically characterize the system. It features a modular mounting system to fix an RX coil at precise coordinates, enabling measurements with fixed amounts of coupling.

On the electronics side, a DC power supply drives an H-bridge, generating the high-frequency alternating current required for the TX tank. An Analog Discovery Pro oscilloscope is used for simultaneous telemetry of voltage and current waveforms across both the TX and RX coils.

Current Status

Experiments have been conducted to characterize the RX coils under various circuit topologies, validating the resonance stability predicted by the simulation. Additionally, system level testing has identified that tuning the operating frequency is the primary driver for maximizing power transfer. All data and analysis is available in the project repository.

To date, we have successfully achieved wireless flight of the full quadcopter configuration after initial tests with a single RX coil. Future efforts will focus on emperically mapping and optimizing the magnetic field well to achieve passive stability.
