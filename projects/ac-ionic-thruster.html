Objective

Ionic thrusters are a form of electric propulsion that generate thrust by accelerating ions in uniform (DC) electric fields. Compared to traditional chemical rockets, ionic thrusters are significantly more efficient. However, their low thrust generation restricts their use to the vacuum of space.

The goal of this project was to investigate the addition of AC technology to ion thrusters, with the aim of increasing thrust and thrust efficiency for potential terrestrial applications. The project was broken down into 4 stages, with simulation and design of both DC and AC thrusters.

Theory

The project was approached from first principles, starting with the physics of ion propulsion. The slides to the left outline the underlying mechanisms and theory that govern ionic thrusters in a terrestrial setting.

The slides also showcase possible AC modifications. These include pulsing the DC voltage to generate more ions while keeping the system efficient, and using a linear accelerator to boost the thrust generated from the ions.

Test Stand Design

To mitigate development risks, we first built a simulation in a Jupyter Notebook. By linking theoretical equations, we modeled the expected thrust and efficiency across various geometries and voltage configurations.

Given the high operating voltages (up to 30kV), safety was the primary driver for the physical test stand design. The test stand underwent multiple iterations to ensure the high-voltage components were properly insulated and shielded. Sketches of the mechanical design and safety circuitry are shown below.

Results

Ultimately, the project was shelved due to cost and safety concerns surrounding the high voltages. Despite this, the research phase provided valuable experience in scoping complex engineering projects and applying systems-level thinking. The work highlights the practical challenges of high-voltage experimentation and establishes a theoretical framework for future AC propulsion studies.
