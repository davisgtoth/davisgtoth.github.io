The Competition

For the annual Engineering Physics Robot Competition, in groups of four, we were tasked with building a fully autonomous robot in just six weeks. Themed after Mario Kart, the challenge required the robot to navigate a "Rainbow Road" circuit complete with shortcuts, competing head-to-head against an opponent.

The objective was to maximize our score within a strict time limit. Points were earned by completing laps, retrieving item boxes, and collecting coins hung on a zipline, while avoiding point-deducting "bombs". Beyond the course itself, the robot faced the added complexity of a dynamic opponent, forcing it to actively avoid collisions while dodging "bananas" and additional bombs dropped to sabotage the track.

System Overview

Meet R.O.B.erto, a custom-built autonomous racer designed to dominate the Rainbow Road. Capable of precise tape following, active bomb avoidance, and item retrieval, the robot features a specialized mechanism to autonomously mount and ride the zipline to gather the coins. R.O.B.erto was the only robot in the entire 16-team competition to successfully complete this complex aerial challenge.

At the core of the system is an STM32 microcontroller acting as the mastermind, executing C++ firmware to control all logic and behavior. The architecture utilizes an interrupt-driven design to handle real-time sensor data from sonars and encoders. To drive the motors, we designed and soldered custom H-bridge PCBs using discrete MOSFETs. Special attention was given to electrical routing to minimize electromagnetic interference, ensuring that high-current motor noise never corrupted the delicate signals from any of the sensors.

Mechanical Design

As mechanical lead, I designed a custom laser-cut hardboard chassis utilizing press-fit joinery to ensure structural rigidity without the use of excessive fasteners. To withstand the rough journey around the course, I integrated 3D-printed mounts that securely positioned the electronics and sensors. The chassis was engineered to efficiently house the motors, batteries, and a cargo bay for collected items, all while adhering to strict size constraints required to navigate the track. Crucially, the frame was reinforced to support the robot's entire weight during the high-stress zipline descent. To complete the race-ready look, the exterior was finished with custom "sponsorship" decals, giving R.O.B.erto a distinct NASCAR-inspired aesthetic.

Navigation & Control

R.O.B.erto's navigation relied on a rear differential drive system balanced by a front caster wheel, enabling tight cornering on the winding course. He tracked the path using a custom PCB housing an array of six infrared reflectance sensors. Combining IR LEDs and phototransistors, these sensors measured the intensity of reflected light, allowing the system to distinguish the absorbent black tape from the reflective white surface.

When R.O.B.erto drifted off-center, the combined sensor readings quantified a positional error that fed into a PID controller. This controller dynamically adjusted the left and right motor speeds to keep him centered on the line. Additionally, a forward-mounted ultrasonic sensor actively scanned for opponents, automatically reducing speed when obstacles were detected to prevent collisions.

Item Box Collection

To retrieve items and score points, R.O.B.erto utilized a custom intake mechanism dubbed the "Elasti-Grab." This system consisted of a rotating wheel fitted with elastic bands that swept boxes into an internal collection bay. Designed for robustness, the intake motor was monitored by a rotary encoder coupled via custom 3D-printed gears. If the system detected a stall, it automatically reversed the motor to clear the jam.

To avoid collecting point-deducting "bombs", the robot used a computer vision pipeline running on a Raspberry Pi to identify the red hazards. To minimize architectural complexity, communication between the Pi and the main STM32 microcontroller was streamlined to a single logic pin. When a bomb was identified, this signal triggered the Elasti-Grab to spin in reverse, rejecting the object to ensure only valid items were collected.

Zipline

The most technically demanding challenge of the race course was the zipline. To tackle this complex problem, we engineered a custom scissor lift, driven by a lead screw and mounted to the rear of the chassis. To simplify the latching process, the hook's geometry was designed to naturally engage the pole as the robot drove towards it. At the top of the lift was a small tray embedded with magnets, positioned to capture and retain the all the coins.

To ensure autonomous operation, a gyroscope was used to detect the incline of the course's ramp. This signal initiated the lift, which was regulated by limit switches for precise positioning. After reaching the end of the line, the robot executed a programmed dismount maneuver to unhook itself and re-acquire the black tape to keep on racing.
